Scoring Rubric

PT Scholarship · 5 categories · 15 points maximum

Score each applicant independently against the rubric — do not compare applicants to each other. Evaluate only what is present in the application; do not infer missing information. Add brief comments for high or low scores — this will help the committee during deliberations.

Personal Essay

3 pts
3
Exceeds
Compelling, well-structured, strong voice. Deep passion for aviation, clear long-term goals, persuasive case for how the scholarship accelerates their path. Polished and engaging.
2
Meets
Addresses the prompt but lacks depth, specificity, or cohesion. Aviation goals present but vague. Understandable but may have noticeable errors or awkward flow.
1
Does Not Meet
Unclear, incomplete, or minimally connected to aviation. Goals missing or generic. Writing quality significantly hinders understanding.

Letters of Recommendation

3 pts
3
Exceeds
2 strong letters that speak specifically to the applicant's character, work ethic, and aviation potential.
1
Does Not Meet
Fewer than 2 letters of recommendation provided.

Resume

3 pts
3
Exceeds
Well-organized and professional. Demonstrates aviation-related experience (training, certifications, flight hours, clubs, competitions, volunteer work, etc.) with clear progression and initiative.
2
Meets
Includes some aviation exposure or experience but lacks depth, detail, or clear achievements. Formatting may be inconsistent.
1
Does Not Meet
Minimal or unclear aviation experience. Resume is poorly organized or incomplete.

3 pts

Reviewer Guidelines

Key principles for fair, consistent, and equitable evaluation

Do

  • Score based only on what is written in the application — no assumptions
  • Review each application independently before comparing with peers
  • Consider the whole applicant, not just one strong or weak area
  • Note your reasoning briefly when giving very high or very low scores
  • Ask the chair if you are unsure how to score a particular situation
  • Remember the goal: find candidates who will make the most of this opportunity

Don't

  • Discuss scores or rankings with other reviewers before you've submitted independently
  • Let one exceptional (or weak) area inflate or deflate unrelated scores
  • Make assumptions about need, background, or identity beyond what's written
  • Apply personal bias for or against a particular school, career path, or writing style
  • Share any applicant information outside the committee
  • Score an applicant you have a personal connection to — recuse yourself

Equity & Bias Awareness

  • Prestige bias: Attending a well-known school does not indicate merit
  • Extracurricular bias: Students working to support their family may have fewer activities — weight quality over quantity
  • Writing style bias: English may not be a first language; evaluate ideas, not polish
  • Recency bias: Don't let a strong final paragraph overshadow a weak overall application
  • When in doubt, ask yourself: "Am I scoring the application, or my assumptions about the person?"
